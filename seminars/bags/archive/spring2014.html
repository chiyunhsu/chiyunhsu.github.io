  • February 112013 Science Center 232Harvard Francesco Cavazzani
    The Bialynicki-Birula decomposition

    Given a variety with an action of the multiplicative group, a beautiful theorem of Bialynicki-Birula from 1973 gives us an affine stratification based on ``limits'' of orbits. It is indeed a very simple and powerful tool, and it can be seen as algebraic analogue of Morse theory. We will describe it, and give many consequences and examples.

    Unirationality of some moduli spaces of curves

  • February 252013 Science Center 232Harvard Nasko Atanasov
    Correspondences and the geometry of space curves The origins of modern intersection theory can be traced back to Schubert calculus and the theory of correspondences. We will introduce correspondences and discuss some of their applications to geometry of space curves. Along the way, we will discuss several enumerative questions about trisecants such as counting the stationary trisecants, tangential trisecants, and quadrisecants.
  • March 42013 Science Center 232Harvard John Sheridan
    Schubert Calculus and Littlewood-Richardson Rules A set of techniques used by Hermann Schubert in his study of enumerative questions in projective geometry during the mid 19th century became what is now know as Schebert calculus. The techniques are used for calculating cohomology classes in certain homogeneous spaces and in particular the Grassmannian. We will look at how these techniques work in the Grassmannian, and we will talk about the rules/algorithms that have arisen to help calculate the Littlewood-Richardson structure coefficients of the Chow ring of this space.
  • March 112013 Science Center 232Harvard Francesco Cavazzani
    Complete twisted cubics Finding characteristic numbers of twisted cubics is a very old problem that can be traced back to Schubert's book "Kalkul der abzahlenden Geometrie" in 1879. Even though many solutions have been proposed since then, we still do not have a completely satisfactory moduli space, expecially when it comes to more intricate prroblems involving tangency or multisecancy conditions. Following the construction of wonderful compactifications by De Concini and Procesi, and taking as model the space of complete conics, we propose a new compactification of the space of twisted cubics; we will show some properties of this space, and discuss why this choice could lead to new characteristic numbers. If time permits, we will describe the geometry in codimension 1 and 2 of the boundary. This is the practice for a talk in Zurich on Friday.

  • March 252013 Science Center 232Harvard Alex Perry
    Mumford-Tate groups Mumford-Tate groups are the basic symmetry groups of Hodge theory. I will define these groups and discuss some of their applications, e.g. to the Hodge conjecture and to obstructing Hodge structures of certain varieties from "coming from" abelian varieties.
  • April 12013 Science Center 232Harvard Eric Riedl
    Rational curves on hypersurfaces On a general hypersurface of degree d in P^n, we expect the dimension of the space of degree e rational curves to be e(n-d+1)+n-4. I will first outline the problem and explain why the expected dimension is reasonable. Then, I will do a ``low-tech'' treatment of some example cases, trying to give an idea of the techniques involved. Finally, I will sketch the proof that the expected dimension equals the actual dimension for n>=d+2.
  • April 82013 Science Center 232Harvard Gabriel Bujokas
    Rational curves on K3 surfaces We will go over Beauville's proof of Yau-Zaslow formula for the number of rational curves in the primitive class of a K3 surface.
  • April 152013 Science Center 232Harvard Atanas Atanasov
    Complements of plane curves and their fundamental groups The problem of computing fundamental groups of plane curve complements has a very rich history. We will motivate the problem and discuss some of the approaches for computing pi_1. The focus of our discussion will be a statement by Zariski that the complement of a nodal curve has abelian pi_1. We will present Zariski's original proof which is based on a theorem by Severi (which remains open), and later complete proofs by Abhyankar-Fulton-Hansen-Deligne.
  • April 222013 Science Center 232Harvard Adrian Zahariuc
    The Noether-Lefschetz Theorem We will go over a proof via degeneration (by Griffiths and Harris) of the Noether-Lefschetz theorem.
  • April 292013 Science Center 232Harvard Matthew Woolf
    The Luroth Problem The most basic birational class of varieties are the rational ones. In practice, though, it is often much easier to show that a variety is dominated by a rational variety. It is therefore natural to ask what the difference is between the class of unirational and rational varieties. For curves, there is no difference. For complex surfaces, there is no difference. Over finite fields, and in higher dimensions, there is a distinction. I will discuss some of the methods used to prove irrationality of unirational varieties.
  • May 62013 Science Center 232Harvard Anand Patel
    An overview of Log Geometry

    I will attempt to do the impossible: I will do some *very* basic concrete examples to get the feel of what log structures are, then give an overview of the usefulness of the language of log geometry in various contexts, all while knowing little about log geometry. Should be a heckler's paradise.
